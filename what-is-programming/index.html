What is programming?

When historians look back on our time here on earth, it's not going to be the computers that they remember. Programming is about a new way of thinking, a way of expressing ourselves which we simply didn't need before the invention of computers.

We've always been able to say "what is". But we've never been very good at saying "how to."

Gerald Sussman, computer science professor at MIT and author of the classic Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs, gives a compelling talk about the role of programming in the grander scheme of things.

In short, we've always had natural languages to describe facts about the world. Most of the knowledge we have can be described as declarative.

Declarative knowledge

Or, what is true

√x is the y such that y² = x and y ≥ 0

Declarative knowledge tells us what, but it doesn't tell us how.

But for thousands of years we've also had algorithms, formal steps describing how to accomplish some goal. We can describe this type of knowledge as imperative.

Imperative knowledge

Or, how to do something

To find an approximation to √x
* Make a guess, G
* Improve the guess by averaging G and x / G
* Keep improving the guess until it is good enough

The difference is that we now have programming languages that allow us to explain ideas in a way that even a dumb machine can understand us.

Programming

Using the Scheme language

We can write the same imperative knowledge about how to computer a square root using a modern programming language. Here's how we would compute a square root using the Scheme language.

(define (sqrt x)
  (define (improve guess)
    (average guess (/ x guess)))
  (define (good-enough? guess)
    (< (abs (- (square guess) x))
       .001))
  (define (try guess)
    (if (good-enough? guess)
        guess
        (try (improve guess))))
  (try 1))

Expressing our ideas as programs forces us to understand them at a deeper level than with natural languages (the language of mathematics included). And that's why programming is hard. Machines are unforgiving, and if we don't understand the solution well enough, the program simply won't work.

But the benefits of learning to code make it worth the struggle. Once you learn how it "feels" to understand a program, there's no going back. You realize there are lots of subjects you don't understand as well as you thought you did, and you start to bring the same mental rigor to them as well. And in the end, you understand things more deeply than you could before you learned to code.
